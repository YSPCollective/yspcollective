The Fragrance Wheel

How scents relate to each other

The fragrance wheel maps scent families and shows how they blend, contrast, and complement. Adjacent families share qualities; opposite families create contrast.

Citrus

The freshest family in perfumery. Citrus notes open most fragrances with immediate brightness and lift. They're among the most volatile notes, meaning they're often the first thing you smell and the first to fade — but their energy sets the tone for everything that follows.

Floral

The most diverse family in fragrance, spanning everything from light and airy to rich and opulent. Floral notes form the heart of countless classic perfumes and are the most widely used accord in feminine and unisex compositions. From dewy rose to heady tuberose — florals are perfumery's most expressive palette.

Woody

Warm, grounding and deeply sensual. Woody notes form the backbone of most oriental and masculine compositions and are responsible for the dry-down — the final phase of a fragrance. Woods provide depth, longevity and a sense of natural comfort. Many iconic Arabian perfumes are built around woody bases like oud, sandalwood and cedarwood.

Oriental

The richest, most complex family in perfumery. Oriental notes are warm, exotic and deeply layered — drawing on resins, spices, incense and rare botanical extracts. This is the heartland of Arabian fragrance, where accords like amber, oud and bakhoor create compositions with legendary longevity and extraordinary sillage.

Fresh

Clean, airy and effortless. Fresh notes are created both synthetically and from natural sources to evoke everything from ocean breezes to crisp linen and morning dew. They're the foundation of lighter, daytime and summer fragrances, and often used to balance heavier oriental or woody compositions.

Gourmand

The most modern family in perfumery, popularised from the 1990s onwards. Gourmand notes reference edible ingredients — vanilla, caramel, coffee, chocolate — creating sweet, comforting and often addictive compositions. They're a signature of many contemporary Arabian and niche fragrances, adding warmth and instant wearability.

Understanding fragrance notes, accords & families

A fragrance is constructed in three layers — top notes, heart notes and base notes. Top notes are the first impression: bright, volatile and short-lived. Heart notes emerge after 15–30 minutes and form the core character of the scent. Base notes are the final, lasting phase — the deepest and most enduring part of the fragrance.

An accord is a harmonious blend of individual notes that combine to create a single recognisable smell — like "amber" or "leather". Most fragrances are described by their dominant accords rather than every single ingredient.

Understanding these building blocks helps you predict whether a fragrance will suit your skin, your style and the occasions you wear it.

What's the difference between a note and an accord? +
A note is a single aromatic ingredient (e.g. bergamot, rose, vetiver). An accord is a blend of several notes that together create a single recognisable smell — for example, an "amber" accord might combine benzoin, labdanum, vanilla and sandalwood. Accords are the building blocks; notes are the raw materials.
Why do fragrances smell different on different people? +
Skin chemistry affects how fragrance molecules interact and evaporate. Skin pH, moisture level, body temperature and diet all influence how a scent develops on your skin. This is why it's always recommended to wear a fragrance for a few hours before deciding — what smells like cedar on one person may read more as musk on another.
What makes Arabian fragrances different? +
Arabian perfumes typically use higher concentrations of fragrance oil (often 20–30%) and lean heavily on warming base notes like oud, amber, musk and sandalwood. They're designed for longevity and presence — many last 10–14 hours on skin and project significantly. The tradition also places greater importance on woody and oriental accords compared to Western perfumery's preference for florals and fresh notes.
What does sillage mean? +
Sillage (pronounced "see-yazh") refers to the trail a fragrance leaves in the air as you move. A high-sillage fragrance fills a room and lingers after you've left; a low-sillage fragrance stays close to the skin. Arabian fragrances are typically known for generous sillage thanks to their oil-rich compositions.
How do I choose a fragrance based on notes? +
Start with the base notes — these define the lasting character of the fragrance. If you love warmth and depth, look for woody and oriental bases. If you prefer clean and light, look for fresh or citrus bases. Then use the heart notes to refine your choice: floral hearts add romance, spicy hearts add complexity. The top notes tell you the opening impression but fade quickly — so don't judge a fragrance in the first 5 minutes.
