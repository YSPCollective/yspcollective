## Lattafa and the Ana Abiyedh Line

Lattafa needs little introduction if you've been following Arabian fragrance over the last few years. The Dubai-based house has built one of the most prolific and consistently well-received catalogues in the accessible fragrance world — and the Ana Abiyedh line is one of their crown jewels.

Ana Abiyedh (meaning "I am white" in Arabic) started as a soft, musky floral and has since grown into a family of flankers, each taking the DNA in a different direction: Rouge, Leather, Scarlet, and now Coral. Each one is genuinely different. Coral is the summer edition — the one you reach for when you want to smell like a good time.

## What It Smells Like

This is a Floral Fruity EDP, launched in 2025, and it wears exactly as the name and colour suggest.

**Top notes: Watermelon, Peach, Orange** — the opening is vivid and immediately joyful. The watermelon leads — it's bright and candy-sweet rather than fresh-cut, which is the right call for a fragrance. The peach adds softness and the orange keeps it from tipping into pure confection. First spray, you'll smile. That's genuinely not something every fragrance can claim.

**Heart notes: Coconut, White Flowers** — as it settles, coconut water rather than sunscreen coconut comes through. Refreshing and milky-light. The white flowers sit underneath adding just enough delicacy to lift it above straight fruit punch territory.

**Base notes: Vanilla, Amber, Musk** — the dry-down is where the longevity kicks in. Warm vanilla and amber anchor everything, giving the fragrance a staying power that surprises for this kind of composition. The musk is clean and skin-close rather than heavy.

The overall effect is best described this way: juicy, happy, carefree — like a beach cocktail that somehow also smells sophisticated. It leans feminine, but the coconut-vanilla base keeps it genuinely wearable by anyone who's comfortable with fruity-sweet.

**Longevity:** expect 6–7 hours on skin, longer on clothing. Projection is moderate — a proper scent bubble rather than a room-filling beast, which suits the character perfectly.

## Who It's For

Coral is unambiguously a warm-weather fragrance. Poolside, beach, festivals, summer evenings, daily daywear from May through September. It's not a fragrance for boardrooms or winter. It knows what it is, and that clarity is part of its appeal.

If you loved D&G L'Impératrice, the Escada summer editions, or anything that plays in the juicy-fruit-with-warmth territory — Coral will feel immediately familiar and better-executed than most of what you've tried at three times the price.

## The Inspired-By Angle: Room 1015 Wavechild

The fragrance community has been consistent on this one: Ana Abiyedh Coral sits in the same world as **Room 1015 Wavechild** — a cult niche watermelon fragrance from the Parisian house that typically retails around €170–180 for 100ml.

The DNA is recognisably shared: watermelon-forward, fruity-aquatic, with a musk dry-down. Where Coral distinguishes itself is in warmth — the peach and vanilla bring a sweetness and depth that Wavechild doesn't have. Reviewers consistently note that Coral is the richer, more approachable interpretation; Wavechild is cooler, more minimal, closer to fresh-cut fruit. Both are excellent. Coral costs a tenth of the price and has a bottle that looks the part on any shelf.

Some reviewers also compare it to **Valentino Born in Roma Coral Fantasy** — and while that comparison holds up less precisely on the nose, the mood and occasion are identical. Valentino's version sits around €80–100. Coral does the same job for under €20.
