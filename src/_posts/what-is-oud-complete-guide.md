Oud. Agarwood. Liquid gold. The most expensive raw material in perfumery, sometimes costing more than gold by weight. And yet — if you've smelled any of the popular Arabian fragrances circulating in Europe right now — you've already encountered it.

Here's everything you need to know.

## What is oud?

Oud — known botanically as agarwood — is a resinous heartwood that forms inside Aquilaria trees when they become infected with a specific mould (*Phialophora parasitica*). The tree's defence response produces a dense, dark resin that saturates the wood over years or even decades. It is this resin-saturated wood — and the oil extracted from it — that becomes oud.

Here's the remarkable thing: not all Aquilaria trees produce oud. Only infected trees produce the resin, and not all infected trees produce it in commercially useful quantities. Across the wild forests of Southeast Asia, India, Bhutan and parts of the Middle East, perhaps one in ten trees contains oud — and high-quality oud is rarer still.

The scarcity is real. Centuries of harvesting have pushed wild oud-producing trees toward endangerment across much of their natural range. The highest quality wild oud — aged, complex, from old-growth trees — is genuinely among the most valuable organic materials on earth. A kilogram of superior wild Cambodian oud can trade for more than €30,000.

This is why most oud used in mainstream perfumery — including in the Arabian fragrances we carry — is produced from sustainably farmed Aquilaria trees or synthesised from aroma molecules that replicate the key facets of natural oud.

## How does oud smell?

Oud is extraordinarily difficult to describe to someone who hasn't smelled it. It is not a single smell but a cluster of qualities.

**Smoky.** There is almost always an element of smoke — reminiscent of incense, of wood char, of something ancient.

**Woody.** Beneath the smoke, oud has a deep, dry woodiness — not the clean pencil-shaving dryness of cedarwood, but something much older and more complex.

**Animalic.** High-quality oud has a distinctly animalic quality — warm, skin-like, slightly musky. This is one of the most divisive aspects for newcomers: some love it immediately, others need time to appreciate it.

**Earthy.** There's a rootedness to oud — a soil, forest floor quality that connects it to something primitive and natural.

**Sweet.** Particularly in aged oud, there is a smooth, almost honeyed sweetness that tempers all the intensity above.

The balance of these elements varies enormously by origin, age, distillation method and quality. Indian oud (Hindi) tends to be more animalic and earthy. Cambodian oud is often cleaner and slightly sweet. Laotian oud is considered by many to be the finest — complex, smooth, deeply layered.

## Types of oud

**Wild oud** — harvested from naturally infected trees. The finest and most expensive. Increasingly rare and subject to CITES trade regulations.

**Cultivated oud** — grown from inoculated Aquilaria trees on sustainable farms, primarily in Vietnam, Malaysia and Thailand. Good quality cultivated oud is excellent. It's what the vast majority of oud fragrance uses.

**Synthetic oud** — laboratory-created molecules that replicate the key aromatic facets of natural oud. Used extensively in mainstream perfumery. Allows consistent, affordable oud performance without the ethical and cost issues of natural sourcing.

**Oud accord** — a blend of synthetic molecules and other ingredients that creates a recognisable oud character. Used in most high-street and many niche fragrances labelled as "oud".

There is no shame in synthetic or accord oud — many extraordinary fragrances are built on them. What matters is the composition as a whole.

## Oud in perfumery

Oud has been central to Arabian perfumery for centuries. In the Gulf tradition, raw oud chips are burned over charcoal as bakhoor — a form of aromatic incense used to scent homes, prayer spaces and clothing. Oud oil (attar) is applied directly to skin as a pure fragrance. Oud-based EDPs have become the defining category of modern Arabian perfumery.

In Western perfumery, oud arrived later — popularised in the early 2000s, with Tom Ford's Oud Wood (2007) often cited as a turning point. Today it appears in countless Western mainstream and niche fragrances, though often in heavily modified form — lighter, softer, less challenging than its Arabian counterparts.

The classic Arabian oud pairing is **oud and rose** — considered by many to be the greatest combination in all of perfumery. The animalic earthiness of oud anchored by the floral opulence of rose. It is timeless, deeply cultural, and extraordinarily beautiful when done well.

Other classic pairings:

**Oud and saffron.** The spicy, leathery quality of saffron amplifies the complexity of oud. A combination that reads as genuinely luxurious.

**Oud and amber.** The warm sweetness of amber rounds and smooths oud, making it more accessible and longer-lasting.

**Oud and musk.** A close, intimate combination that keeps oud skin-close rather than projecting it across a room.

## Finding good oud in Europe

The challenge for European buyers has historically been access. Arabian oud fragrances — the genuine article, sourced properly — were difficult to find outside of specialist Middle Eastern fragrance stores. What was available through mainstream channels was often grey market, incorrectly stored or outright fake.

This is changing. Authorised EU distributors now bring genuine Arabian oud fragrances — from houses like Lattafa, Armaf, Arabiyat Prestige and Swiss Arabian — directly into the European market. Every bottle correctly imported, correctly labelled, within date.

When buying oud fragrance in Europe, look for:

- **Authorised EU stock** — the fragrance should have EU-compliant labelling, including ingredient listings
- **Clear batch information** — genuine products have lot numbers and batch codes
- **A traceable seller** — avoid unknown marketplace sellers with no history or reviews
