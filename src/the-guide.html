Fragrance Concentration

EDP, EDT, Parfum — what's the difference?

The biggest single factor in how long a fragrance lasts and how much it projects is its concentration — the percentage of fragrance oil relative to alcohol and water. Higher concentration means more longevity, more projection, and usually a higher price. But it doesn't always mean better — some compositions are designed specifically for a lighter touch.

Application

Where — and how — to wear fragrance

The way you apply fragrance has an enormous impact on how it performs and evolves. Fragrance needs heat to develop — apply to pulse points where blood vessels run close to the skin surface, and let the warmth carry the scent upward.

1
Wrists — Primary pulse point

The classic application spot. The radial artery runs close to the surface here, creating warmth that helps fragrance bloom and project. Apply 1–2 sprays and let it develop naturally — avoid rubbing the wrists together, which breaks down the fragrance molecules and flattens the top notes.

2
Neck / Base of throat

The warmest area of the body and the most projecting application point. Fragrance sprayed here rises naturally with body heat, creating a scent trail as you move. Ideal for evening wear and occasions where you want to be noticed.

3
Chest / Sternum

A powerful point for projection — the heat of your body carries the scent outward and upward. Particularly effective for richer oriental and oud-based fragrances. This is where Arabian fragrances traditionally excel.

4
Behind the ears

The warmth behind the ear creates a close, intimate cloud of scent — perfect for evenings. Fragrance applied here tends to last particularly well. Also the ideal spot for dabs of perfume oil or attar.

5
Inner elbows

Underused but effective — a warm, sheltered pulse point that allows fragrance to develop steadily. Great for hot weather when wrists become sweaty and alter the scent.

6
Hair

Hair holds fragrance beautifully and creates a trail as you move. Spray into the air and walk through the mist, or apply to a hairbrush. Fragrance oils are better suited to hair than alcohol-based sprays.

The rule of thumb

For EDP, 3–5 sprays is usually enough. More is not better — it becomes overwhelming and the composition loses definition. For perfume oil / attar, 1–2 dabs per pulse point. Oils are slower to project but longer lasting — resist the urge to apply more in the first hour.

Understanding Arabian Fragrance

Arabian perfume vs Western fragrance

Arabian and Western perfumery traditions are fundamentally different in philosophy, ingredients and performance. Understanding these differences helps you appreciate why fragrances like Lattafa, Armaf and Swiss Arabian behave the way they do — and why they represent extraordinary value.

Arabian Perfumery
Dubai · UAE · Gulf tradition
  • ◆Built on oud, amber, musk, saffron and rich spice accords
  • ◆Oil concentration typically 20–30% — well above Western EDP
  • ◆Longevity of 10–16 hours is standard, not exceptional
  • ◆Designed to project — sillage is considered a sign of quality
  • ◆Often unisex or gender-fluid in character
  • ◆Oud, bakhoor and rose are the holy trinity of Arabian fragrance
  • ◆Price reflects materials, not marketing — exceptional value
  • ◆Scent develops slowly — judge at 2–3 hours, not immediately
Western Perfumery
Paris · London · New York tradition
  • ◇Built on florals, fresh notes, woods and clean musks
  • ◇EDP typically 15–20% oil concentration
  • ◇Longevity of 4–8 hours typical for EDP
  • ◇Restrained projection preferred — "close to the skin" is valued
  • ◇Strongly gendered marketing and positioning
  • ◇Bergamot, jasmine, vetiver and cedarwood are the classic backbone
  • ◇Price includes significant marketing, packaging and brand premium
  • ◇Top notes important — designed to impress immediately
The value question

A bottle of Creed Aventus costs €300–400. A bottle of Armaf Club de Nuit Intense — widely acknowledged as the closest affordable alternative — costs under €30. The gap in performance? Most people who've worn both would struggle to call it significant. Arabian fragrance houses have access to the same raw material suppliers and often outperform on longevity.

This is why Arabian fragrances have exploded in popularity among fragrance enthusiasts globally — not as "cheap alternatives" but as genuinely excellent compositions that happen to be priced fairly.

Advanced Technique

Layering fragrances

Layering — wearing two or more fragrances together — is a traditional Arabian practice and one of the most creative aspects of perfumery. Done well, it creates a completely personal scent that belongs only to you.

01
Foundation
Apply the base first

Start with your richest, heaviest fragrance — typically your oud, amber or oriental base. Apply to pulse points and let it settle for 2–3 minutes before adding anything over it. The foundation fragrance should be the dominant character.

02
Complement
Add your top layer

Apply your lighter, fresher fragrance over the top — a floral, citrus or fresh accord that lifts the base and adds complexity. The two should enhance each other, not compete. Start with one spray and evaluate before adding more.

03
Patience
Let it develop

Layered fragrances take 20–30 minutes to fully merge on skin. Reserve your final judgement for at least half an hour after application — what smells jarring initially often becomes beautifully integrated once the alcohol burns off.

The safest rule: fragrances from the same family layer beautifully. Two orientals, two woods, a floral over a woody base — these combinations create depth without conflict. Classic combinations: oud + rose (the Arabian classic), vanilla + wood (sweet but grounded), citrus over amber (fresh brightness over warm depth), lavender + musk (clean and effortless). Avoid combining two dominant, competing characters.

Skincare

The K-beauty routine — order matters

Korean skincare philosophy is built on layering products from lightest to heaviest, allowing each to absorb before adding the next. The order you apply products in directly affects their efficacy — apply in the wrong sequence and you can block absorption. Here's the correct sequence.

1
AM + PM
Oil Cleanser — First cleanse

An oil-based cleanser dissolves oil-soluble impurities: sunscreen, makeup, sebum and pollution. Massage onto dry skin, add a little water to emulsify, then rinse. This is not optional if you wear SPF or makeup — water-based cleansers cannot remove these effectively on their own.

Anua Heartleaf Pore Control Cleansing Oil
2
AM + PM
Water-Based Cleanser — Second cleanse (PM)

After oil cleansing, a gentle water-based cleanser removes remaining residue and water-soluble impurities. In the morning, a light rinse with water is usually sufficient — skin doesn't accumulate the same impurities overnight.

3
AM + PM
Toner / Essence — Hydration prep

Applied to slightly damp skin, a hydrating toner or essence begins the moisture layering process and preps skin to better absorb subsequent products. Pat in gently — do not rub.

4
AM + PM
Serum — Active ingredients

Serums deliver concentrated active ingredients — hyaluronic acid for hydration, niacinamide for pores and brightness, PDRN for repair and regeneration. Apply to damp skin and pat in gently. If using multiple serums, apply from thinnest to thickest consistency.

Anua PDRN Hyaluronic Acid Capsule 100 Serum
5
AM + PM
Moisturiser — Seal and protect

A moisturiser locks in the hydration and actives applied in previous steps and creates a protective barrier. Apply while skin is still slightly damp from serum for best absorption. In the morning, choose a lighter texture; PM can be richer and more occlusive.

Anua Heartleaf 70 Intense Calming Cream
6
AM only
SPF — Non-negotiable

Sunscreen is the single most impactful anti-ageing product you can use. Apply as the final step of your morning routine, after moisturiser, before makeup. SPF must be the last skincare layer to function correctly. Do not mix it into your moisturiser. Reapply every 2 hours if outdoors.

Common Questions

Everything else you might want to know

Skin chemistry — pH, moisture level, body temperature, diet and even medication — all affect how fragrance molecules interact and evaporate from your skin. A fragrance that reads as sweet vanilla on one person can smell powdery and dry on another. This is normal and expected. It's why wearing a fragrance for several hours before buying is important.

Not necessarily the fridge, but away from light, heat and humidity. The bathroom — despite being the most common storage spot — is actually the worst place (heat and steam degrade fragrance over time). Store bottles in a cool, dark place: a bedroom drawer, cupboard, or dedicated fragrance box. For everyday fragrances, simply avoid direct sunlight and heat.

Signs of a degraded fragrance: the colour has darkened significantly, the opening smells sour or vinegary, or the characteristic scent has dramatically flattened. Most fragrances stored correctly last 5–10 years — the alcohol acts as a preservative. Fragrances with high citrus or fresh content degrade faster than oriental or woody compositions.

Sillage (pronounced "see-yazh") is a French word meaning "wake" — as in the wake left behind a boat. In fragrance, it refers to the trail of scent you leave as you move through a room. Arabian fragrances are typically known for generous sillage — considered a sign of quality in the Gulf tradition.

Natural ingredients are extracted directly from plant, animal or mineral sources. Synthetic ingredients are laboratory-created molecules — either to replicate naturals or to create scents that don't exist in nature. Synthetic doesn't mean inferior — most modern perfumes are a blend of both. The key question is always: does it smell good and does it work?

Avoid rubbing — this generates heat that distorts the composition.
